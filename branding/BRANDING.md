---

### Target Audience

**Primary:** Self-taught and intermediate infrastructure engineers (3–8 years experience) who are comfortable with Linux/CLI but haven't committed to a single cloud platform. They're curious about multi-cloud, automation, and AI agents—and they're allergic to tutorials that skip the teardown and leave them with a $200 bill.

**Secondary:** CS/DevOps students and bootcamp grads who want to see real IaC patterns (not click-ops walkthroughs) and engineers exploring AI agent infrastructure for the first time.

**Not the audience:** Enterprise architects making purchasing decisions, or total beginners who've never touched a terminal.

---

### Voice & Tone Guide

**The character:** A senior infra engineer who genuinely enjoys this stuff and doesn't gatekeep. Competent without being pompous. Funny without being a YouTuber persona. Think: someone who writes good commit messages AND good jokes in the PR description.

**Do:**
- Use real CLI output. Show the command, show the error, show the fix.
- Name things precisely ("flux-1-schnell" not "an AI model").
- Be irreverent about cloud vendor marketing, reverential about cost math.
- Use the talon/raptor metaphor naturally ("strike fast, clean kill, no leftovers").
- Acknowledge when something is annoying to set up.
- End every demo with the destroy step—celebrate it, don't skip it.
- Write titles that are statements, not questions ("We Deployed a Telegram Bot on Oracle Free Tier and You Can Too" > "Can You Deploy...?")

**Don't:**
- Use corporate buzzwords (synergy, leverage, holistic, journey, ecosystem).
- Promote specific vendors as sponsors without disclosure.
- Claim anything is "simple" or "easy"—say "reproducible" instead.
- Leave infrastructure running in any tutorial without explicitly noting the cost.
- Use clickbait titles that contradict the video content.
- Name, imply, or hint at any real employer, client, or internal system.

## 3. Visual Identity

> **CHOSEN: Direction 2 — "Terminal Raptor" (neon cyan on black).** This is what shipped (logo, avatar, banner, favicons). The original draft below listed Direction 1 as "recommended" — that recommendation is **superseded**. ### Direction 1 — "Precision Strike" (NOT chosen — superseded by Direction 2)

**The Mark:** A geometric falcon/raptor head in profile, rendered as a minimal 3–4 polygon flat icon. The beak is sharp and forward-facing. Overlaid inside the chest/body area is a tiny circuit-trace or terminal-bracket `>_` motif — subtle, only visible at 64px+. At 16px, it reads as a clean raptor silhouette.

**Talon + Bot Idea:** The raptor IS the bot — the circuit trace inside the body makes it mechanical without being clunky. The talons (feet, visible at larger sizes) grip a data cable or ethernet wire.

**Why it works:** Immediately distinctive at thumbnail scale. The raptor silhouette is readable at 16px favicon. The circuit overlay rewards closer inspection (good for merch, channel art). Works in both dark and light contexts.

**Where it shines:** Favicon: raptor head only, single color. Banner: full body with talons + cable motif on a dark background. Thumbnail watermark: head silhouette, 20% opacity.

**Color Palette:**
```
Primary:     #1A1A2E  (deep space navy — backgrounds)
Accent:      #E94560  (strike red — the talon / active elements)
Highlight:   #0F3460  (electric blue-indigo — secondary text, borders)
Terminal:    #00FF88  (phosphor green — CLI snippets, code blocks)
Light bg:    #F5F5F5  (light mode fallback)
```

**Typography:**
- Display/logo: **JetBrains Mono** (Google Fonts) — monospace with personality, instantly signals "this is a code channel"
- Titles/headlines: **Space Grotesk** — techy geometric sans, pairs cleanly with the mono
- Body/descriptions: **Inter** — maximally readable, never distracts

**Mascot:** YES — propose "Talon" (the mascot), a stylized mechanical falcon. Not cartoonish, not full-furry. Think: vector art bird with visible panel lines and a blinking terminal cursor for an eye. Lives in the channel intro animation, reacts to events (applies code, watches terraform destroy, gives a thumbs-up). Optional for MVP — can be added in Season 2 when the channel has traction.

### Naming Risks to Watch

1. **"TalonBots" vs "Talon Bots"** — Pick one and be consistent. Recommended: one word (`TalonBots`) everywhere. Two words reads as a product category, not a brand.

2. **"Labs" suffix** — Some platforms have character limits. `TalonBotsLabs` (12 chars) is under most limits. `TalonBots Labs` (space) may not work as a handle everywhere — the no-space version is the canonical handle form.

3. **YouTube handle availability** — Check `@TalonBotsLabs` now. YouTube handle squatting has increased significantly. If it's taken by an inactive channel, you can report via YouTube's handle policy after channel creation.

4. **"TalonBots" trademark** — Do a USPTO search for "TalonBots" before any merch or LLC business use. The name is distinctive enough to be protectable but unique enough that conflicts are unlikely.

5. **talonbots.com ownership** — Already owned (confirmed). Ensure DNS for the main domain points to the channel/landing page, not a parked page, before the first video goes live. First impressions matter.

6. **GitHub org vs personal account** — Ensure `github.com/TalonBots` is a proper *organization*, not a personal account, so contributors can be added later and repos can have team permissions.

7. **Consistency across repo naming** — Stick to the `[series-tag]-[ep#]-[slug]` convention from day one. Renaming GitHub repos breaks links in video descriptions.

8. **AI/Agent naming overlap** — "TalonBots" + "Labs" + "bot" has natural SEO overlap with "bot" spam and crypto bot channels. The IaC/infrastructure angle is the differentiator — lean into it in titles and descriptions to avoid algorithmic grouping with low-quality bot content.
